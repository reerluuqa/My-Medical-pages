Transforming traditional guidance into a structured, visual, and interactive web app. Based on NICE CKS principles.

RED FLAGS: Systemic & Complicated Infection (REFER IMMEDIATELY)

Refer or seek urgent specialist advice if you suspect spread of infection or systemic illness, especially in immunocompromised individuals:

  • **Systemic Signs:** Fever, tachycardia, significant pain disproportionate to findings.
  • **Deep Space Infection:** Suspected **Septic tenosynovitis** (e.g., severe pain on passive extension), **Whitlow** (infection of the pulp space), or **Osteomyelitis**.
  • **Complicated Spread:** Infection extending to both nail folds (**Runaround infection**) or signs of **Septic arthritis**.

CAUTION: Etiology & Key Risk Factors

Acute Paronychia is typically bacterial, caused by ***Staphylococcus aureus*** (most common) or Streptococci, usually following minor trauma to the cuticle.

Exacerbating Factors: Diabetes mellitus, Immunosuppression (medication/condition), exposure to irritants (chemicals, excessive moisture), and aggressive nail care (manicuring, nail biting).

Clinical Management Pathway: Steps & Decisions

1
Initial Assessment: Confirm localized painful swelling of the nail fold (onset 2-5 days post-trauma). Rule out red flags (Step 0 - See Red Card).
**Decision Point:** Is there evidence of a **Fluctuant Pus Collection (Abscess)**?
Check for distinct fluctuance or obvious pointing of pus under the nail fold.
**YES** (Abscess)
**NO** (Minor/No Pus)
2A
Management: Incision and Drainage (I&D)
Drain the pus (usually requires elevation or partial avulsion of the nail fold).
Antibiotics: Prescribe a 5–7 day course of oral antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin) *post-I&D*, or if surrounding cellulitis is present.
2B
Management: Conservative Self-Care
Advise warm water soaks 3-4 times a day, maintaining hand hygiene, and avoiding immersion/trauma.
3
Follow-Up / Re-assessment:
* **Schedule Re-check:** Advise patient to return in **48 hours** if symptoms worsen or do not begin to improve with self-care/antibiotics.
* **Antibiotics Failed:** If no improvement after 48–72 hours of appropriate oral antibiotics, **re-assess for complications** (Red Flags) or consider changing to a broader spectrum antibiotic (e.g., Co-amoxiclav) if cultures are not possible.

Key Treatment and Information

Essential Self-Care

Soaking: Warm water (or saline) soaks for 10–20 minutes, 3–4 times daily.

Protection: Keep hands clean and dry, especially after soaking. Wear gloves for wet work.

First-Line Oral Antibiotic

Drug: **Flucloxacillin** (Staph. coverage).

Duration: **5–7 days** course is typically sufficient.

Dose: 500mg QDS (Check local guidelines and patient allergies).

Penicillin Allergy Alternative

Alternative: **Clarithromycin** or **Erythromycin**.

Caution: Check for drug-to-drug interactions (e.g., statins, anticoagulants) with macrolides like Clarithromycin/Erythromycin.

Detailed Notes and Clinical Context

Differential Diagnosis

Ensure correct diagnosis by considering:

  • Herpetic Whitlow: Usually clustered vesicles (blisters) rather than the solid, painful swelling of acute paronychia.
  • Chronic Paronychia: Often affects multiple digits, is less acutely painful, and is commonly associated with Candida (fungal) infection.
  • Psoriatic Nail Disease: Chronic changes, not an acute infection.
Need for Swabs and Culture

Routine swabbing is **not recommended** for simple, uncomplicated acute paronychia as the pathogen is predictable.

Swabs are indicated if:

  • Infection is persistent or recurrent.
  • Initial empirical antibiotic therapy has failed.
  • Patient is immunocompromised.
  • Organism other than *S. aureus* is suspected (e.g., following water exposure).
