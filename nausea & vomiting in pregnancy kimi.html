Nausea & Vomiting in Pregnancy

Clinical Management Algorithm | Last revised: November 2025

Assessment & Initial Evaluation

Key Questions to Ask

  • Onset, duration, frequency, and severity of symptoms
  • Impact on oral intake and hydration status
  • Onset after 11 weeks suggests alternative cause
  • Sleep pattern in 24-hour period
  • Mood, emotional wellbeing, and coping ability
  • Impact on daily functioning and quality of life
  • Additional symptoms (weight loss, fever, pain, neurological)
  • Previous history, family history, medications
  • Comorbidities (diabetes, CKD)

Clinical Examination

  • Temperature (fever suggests alternative cause)
  • Blood pressure, pulse, dehydration assessment
  • Oxygen saturation and respiratory rate
  • Weight check for loss/muscle wasting
  • Abdominal examination for pain/tenderness
  • Neurological signs (Wernicke's encephalopathy)

Severity Scoring

PUQE-24 Score

PUQE Score Interpretation: Mild ≤6, Moderate 7-12, Severe 13-15. Score ≥13 suggests hospital admission.

Investigations to Consider

Mild Symptoms

No additional investigations needed if uncomplicated

Moderate-Severe
  • Mid-stream urine (UTI screen)
  • Pelvic ultrasound (multiple/molar pregnancy)
  • Blood tests: FBC, U&E, glucose
Reassurance: Mild-to-moderate symptoms are common and usually resolve by 16-20 weeks.
Differential Diagnosis
Consider alternative causes if symptoms start after 11 weeks or are atypical

Treatment Management

Non-Pharmacological Measures

First-line for mild symptoms
  • Rest as needed, avoid sensory triggers (odours, heat, noise)
  • Plain biscuits/crackers in morning
  • Small, frequent, protein-rich, high-carb, low-fat meals
  • Cold meals if smell-related nausea
  • Drink little and often
  • Acupressure at P6 point (wrist band/finger pressure)
  • Avoid iron preparations if exacerbating symptoms
  • Manage reflux symptoms separately

Drug Treatment Algorithm

First-Line Start Here

Cyclizine
50 mg orally up to 3×/day

Notes: Antihistamine. Reassess 24-72h. Continue if effective.

Caution: Hepatic impairment, glaucoma, epilepsy

Promethazine
20-25 mg at bedtime (may repeat in AM)

Notes: Antihistamine. Sedating. Avoid driving.

Caution: CNS depression, antimuscarinic effects

Prochlorperazine
5-10 mg up to 3×/day (oral) OR 3-6 mg buccal 2×/day

Notes: Phenothiazine. Avoid in Parkinson's.

Chlorpromazine
10-15 mg every 4-6 hours

Notes: Phenothiazine. Monitor for drowsiness.

Doxylamine/Pyridoxine (Xonvea®)
20/20 mg at bedtime, titrate to max 40/40 mg/day

Notes: Only licensed drug for NVP. Only >18 years.

Preferred option where available

Second-Line If first-line ineffective

Metoclopramide
5-10 mg up to 8-hourly

Max duration: 5 days (risk of extrapyramidal effects)

Short-term use only

Avoid >5 days

Domperidone
10 mg up to 3×/day

Max duration: 7 days (cardiac risk)

Cardiac monitoring needed

Avoid in cardiac disease

Ondansetron
4-8 mg 6-8 hourly

Max duration: 5 days typically

Small ↑ risk oral cleft in 1st trimester

Use if first-line ineffective

Third-Line Specialist advice

Prednisolone
40-50 mg daily, taper to lowest effective dose

Rescue therapy when standard treatments fail. Use with ongoing antiemetics.

Monitor BP & glucose regularly

Adjunctive Treatments

  • H2 blockers/PPIs: For reflux symptoms
  • Thiamine: If severely reduced intake (prevent Wernicke's)
  • Laxatives: If constipation from antiemetics
  • VTE prophylaxis: Assess risk in severe cases

Monitoring & Follow-up

  • Review 24-72 hours after starting treatment
  • Weekly review if responding
  • Taper medication gradually when symptoms improve (12-16 weeks)
  • Early discontinuation may cause relapse
  • Future pregnancies: start lifestyle measures early
Referral & Admission Criteria
Hospital Admission Indicators

Red Flags - Arrange Urgent Admission

Clinical Dehydration

Dry mucous membranes, tachycardia, postural hypotension, oliguria

IV fluids required

Weight Loss

>5% body weight loss

Malnutrition risk

PUQE Score

Score ≥13 indicates severity

Admission threshold

Unable to Tolerate Oral

Cannot keep down antiemetics or fluids

IV antiemetics needed

Medication Issues

Cannot take essential medications (e.g., antibiotics, diabetes meds)

Risk of complications

Mental Health Concerns

Depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation

5% risk of termination

7% suicidal ideation

Specialist Advice Triggers

  • Symptoms not controlled with primary care management
  • Need for third-line therapy (steroids)
  • Suspected hyperemesis gravidarum despite oral treatment
  • Suspected serious complication
  • Need for IV/IM antiemetics
  • Consideration of nutritional support (TPN)

Hyperemesis Gravidarum Definition

Severe nausea/vomiting causing inability to eat/drink normally, strongly limiting daily activities, with signs of dehydration.
Requires specialist management
May need hospital admission

HELP Score (Alternative Severity Tool)

HELP Score: None/mild ≤19, Moderate 20-32, Severe 33-60

Maternal Complications to Monitor

Metabolic

Weight loss, electrolyte imbalance, AKI, abnormal LFTs, Wernicke's encephalopathy

Thiamine supplementation if prolonged

Mechanical

GERD, Mallory-Weiss tears, pneumothorax

VTE Risk

Dehydration + immobility = increased VTE risk

Assess VTE risk

Psychological

Depression, anxiety, PTSD, reduced quality of life

Screen mental health

Fetal Considerations

  • Mild-moderate NVP: No negative impact, may be marker of healthy pregnancy
  • Hyperemesis with low weight gain: ↑ risk preterm delivery, low birthweight
  • No significant association with congenital abnormalities
  • No association with perinatal/neonatal mortality
